01 Public health research institute

We study how material conditions and meaning-making couple to shape health.

Public health measures material conditions with care. It measures meaning with a checkbox. Material & Meaning treats spiritual and secular life as a determinant of survival, measured the way the field already measures housing and income.

The question we start with

Does a county's religious ecology change how steeply rent burden tracks deaths of despair, and which kind of religious presence buffers the toll while another sharpens it?

02 The object

One research object: two poles and the coupling between them.

Pole one / Material

Rent and housing cost, wages, cost of living, incarceration, psychiatric holds. The conditions a body lives inside.

Pole two / Meaning

What people believe they are for. Belief, practice, the secular position. The atheist and the agnostic are positions here, not absences. Saying no is still an answer.

The coupling. The novel work is neither pole on its own. It is the coupling. Material precarity reshapes meaning-making, and meaning-making buffers or worsens health under that precarity. Public health studies the first pole well and the second pole thinly. We measure the second with the seriousness the field gives to housing.

The lens. We read belief and material conditions as one coupled system that co-evolves, through complexity theory and game theory. We look for phase changes. The point where material stress flips a meaning-frame, and the point where a meaning-frame holds a body together that the conditions would otherwise break.

03 The first study / Pilot 01

Rent burden, religious ecology, and deaths of despair.

Some places cost a lot to live in. Rent takes a large share of what people earn. In those places, more people die from suicide, alcohol, and drug overdose. Researchers call these deaths of despair.

Hold the money pressure fixed. Where rent is crushing, does the religious life of a place change what that pressure does to people? And does every kind of congregation act the same, or do some help while others do not?

Why it is not obvious

You might guess religion always helps. It does not. A study across almost 3,000 counties found that some kinds of religious presence went with fewer deaths of despair, and one kind went with more, in the hardest-hit places. The honest question is which kind, where, and under what pressure.

How we measure it

We join open county data. Rent burden comes from the Census. Deaths of despair come from the CDC, pooled over five years so small counties hold up against noise. Religious ecology comes from the US Religion Census. We adjust for income, poverty, joblessness, education, race, and the state a county sits in. We lock the analysis plan before we look. We publish the code and the cleaned data.

What we are honest about

This is about places, not people. Belonging to a congregation is not the same as believing, and we say so. A correlation is not a cause, so we run a stricter test over time and a falsification test on cancer deaths to catch ourselves.

Pre-registered and scoped. We have written the design, the pre-registration, and the data manifest. The next step is the first data pull.

04 Open gaps / For collaborators

Questions open to take.

We keep a ranked list of novel, fundable questions at this nexus. A few are open now.

  1. 01

    Which religious ecology buffers which material shock, and which one sharpens it.

  2. 02

    A county-level estimate of belief content, not just institutional adherence. The field needs it and no one owns it.

  3. 03

    Whether material security predicts secularization at the US county level, with a threshold rather than a smooth slope.

  4. 04

    A natural-experiment design that moves the coupling from association toward cause.

Who we look for

Researchers in population health, the sociology of religion, the cultural evolution of cooperation, and the economics of religion. People comfortable with open data, pre-registration, and saying what the data cannot show.

How we work

Open data. Open methods. Public working papers. Pre-registered analysis. The paper is the artifact and the credibility test.
